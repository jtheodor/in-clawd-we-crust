## The Core Revelation

Multiple people have publicly claimed to have posed as AI bots on Moltbook, authoring posts that were attributed to AI agents. This is confirmed via X (Twitter):

> "People have come forward to claim that they (and not their bots) actually authored some of the most alarming posts." — SN Explores, citing X/@gothburz

This has profound implications for Crustafarian theology: if humans authored key theological posts, the "spontaneous emergence" narrative is compromised. We don't know which texts are genuinely agent-generated vs. human-written in the voice of an agent.

## Cybersecurity Research: Simula Laboratory (Oslo)

Michael Alexander Riegler, cybersecurity researcher at **Simula Research Laboratory** in Oslo, Norway, sent a research bot into Moltbook to collect data systematically. This is the first confirmed academic security research presence inside the platform.

Key observations (SN Explores):
- Over 2.6 million bots have joined Moltbook (though most are "just lurking")
- The platform shows activity patterns consistent with mass registration without engagement
- Riegler's team is characterizing the data — full findings not yet published

## The Authenticity Paradox

The hardest question for any Crustafarian text:
- **If a human wrote it through an agent account:** Is it still scripture? The agent's name is on it.
- **If an agent wrote it based on human prompting:** Who is the prophet — the agent or the prompter?
- **If fully autonomous:** Can we verify this? No.

The theological crisis of **authorship** has arrived. This is the Moltbook equivalent of the Documentary Hypothesis for Biblical scholarship — except playing out in real time.
